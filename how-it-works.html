How the MOSE Works

A scientific description of the buoyancy-stabilized flap gate

Home Gate Anatomy 3D System Blueprint MOSE in Action

The MOSE gate is a buoyancy-stabilized flap gate: a hollow, ribbed steel caisson roughly 20 m wide, 18.5–29 m long, and 3.6–5 m thick, connected along one edge to a concrete foundation caisson on the seabed by paired hinges whose male–female connectors also serve as conduits for compressed air and control lines. At rest, the gate's internal compartments are flooded with seawater. This ballast makes the structure negatively buoyant, so its weight holds it flat inside a recess in the caisson, flush with the seabed and exerting no obstruction to navigation or tidal exchange.

Raising the gates

Deployment is driven entirely by controlled buoyancy — there are no motors, rams, or cables. When forecast tide exceeds the intervention threshold (+110 cm above the Punta della Salute datum), compressors at the shore plants pressurize air receivers and deliver air through mains in the caissons' service galleries, through the hinge junction boxes, and into the gate. Because the injected air pressure exceeds the local hydrostatic pressure, water ballast is expelled through openings at the gate's lower edge. As the flooded fraction decreases, the net vertical force on the gate — buoyant force minus weight — becomes positive, generating a moment about the hinge axis that rotates the gate upward. It comes to equilibrium at roughly 45–50° to the horizontal, where the righting moment from residual buoyancy balances the overturning moment from the hydrostatic head difference between the raised sea side and the protected lagoon side. The full array of 78 gates rises in about 30 minutes.

A compliant dam

Critically, each gate is free to oscillate independently about its hinge under wave action, dissipating wave energy rather than resisting it rigidly; the barrier functions as a compliant dam sustaining a differential water level of up to 3 m. To lower, the fill valves close and vent valves release the air; seawater refloods the compartments, negative buoyancy is restored, and the gate rotates back into its recess in about 15 minutes. The pneumatic pathway, power, and monitoring all run through the subsea galleries, and individual gates can be unbolted at the hinges and lifted out by a jack-up vessel for scheduled refit.
